Autonomism

example, Peter Lamarque ref Art, Emotion and Ethics by Charles Repp, 2009 ASAGE Vol 1 Issue 2

Moral Value is irrelavant to artistic value. So there is no relationship between art and morality in Autonomism. The ones hold this position deny any connection between moral and aesthetic merit in art.

Moralism

example, Berys Gaut ref Art, Emotion and Ethics by Charles Repp, 2009 ASAGE Vol 1 Issue 2

This group is in the othe end of the Autonomists. They believe if the art is morally flawed, it is then artistically flawed (it is not considered art at all). To this group art and morality are not only relevant but one does not exist without the other.

Moderate Moralism

example, Noël Carroll ref Art, Emotion and Ethics by Charles Repp, 2009 ASAGE Vol 1 Issue 2

So these group believe that moral value is SOMETIMES relevant to artistic value. The problem with this idea is that there is no definite way to determine when and to what extend art and morality connect. It all seems very subjective.

Cognitive Immoralism

example, Matthew Kieran, Daniel Jacobsonref Art, Emotion and Ethics by Charles Repp, 2009 ASAGE Vol 1 Issue 2

The follower of cognitive immoralism are trying to find a middle ground (like the moderate moralists) BUT they are more clear on how to determine when and to what extend art and morality connect. To them when immoral aspects help to nurture an understanding or to enhance a culture, then they are not to be viewed as artistic defects. They will in fact add to artistic values.

When art might be considered immoral?

There seem to be a pattern of what some might consider an immoral artwork. Should the artist's moral failing affect the value of his creation? What if the content of an artwork is a matter of immoral subject? Does it matter how the art has been created? What is the message behind the artwork?
